  • Types of Communication

     

    Types of Communication

    Verbal - The sharing of information between two or more individuals, essentially involving speech

     

    Non verbal - Sharing of information by using non-verbal modes like a glance, a gesture, facial expression, posture, smell, etc

     

    Intrapersonal Communication - This is a process of communicating with oneself. Please also see cognitive dissonance theory

     

    Interpersonal communication - An interchange of ideas or information between two or more people

     

    Group Communication - Communication that occurs within a group of people

     

    Mass communication - Communication which is meant for the masses

     

    Mass-line communication - A Maoist strategy of communication evolved during the Chinese revolution and involved propaganda

  • The Theory of Rasa – Indian perspectives on communication

     

    The Theory of Rasa – Indian perspectives on communication  Rasa is the central principle of Indian dramatics and poetics. रस्यतइतिरस: - that which can be tasted (आस्वाद) is Rasa. This is the definition of Rasa. 

    There are six kinds of Rasas – कटू (bitter), तिक्त (spicy), कषाय (acrid), मधुर (sweet), लवण (salty), आम्ल (sour).These Rasas can only be experienced by the sense of taste. The food that is made up of all these Rasas is called the six-Rasa-food or षड्रसभोजन. The same idea of taste is applied to drama and poetry. Thus drama and poetry is supposed to have the same 'tastefulness' and hence Rasas. The eight primary Rasas are शृंगार (beauty), हास्य (laughter), करूण (sadness), रौद्र (anger), वीर (bravery), भयानक (dangerous), बीभत्स (obscene), अद्भुत (miraculous).

    Various theorists of Dramatics have propounded more or less Rasas. It is believed that Bharata's Natyashastra is the first work that discusses the form of Rasa process. However, according to Dr G T Deshpande, before Bharata, two Acharyas Druhin and Vasuki also propounded two different Rasa traditions. Bharata restricts himself to the Rasas in Drama.   

    Theory Application/Relevance:The Rasa theory is probably the oldest theories that explain communication as well as the connection between a performer and an audience. The theory clearly states that the Rasa resides in the Rasik, one who partakes of the communication experience. Only when a performer and the Rasik are on a common plane is communication is completed.

  • Argumentation Theory

    Theorist: Stephen E. Toulmin is the most influential theorist who wrote on 'The Uses of Argument' in 1958, although the origins of argumentation lie in Aristotle's logic propounded way before the 19th century.    

     

    Theory Development: Various versions of the theory were developed over the years, among them Perelman who developed The New Rhetoric in 1969, P.J. Schellens (1985), Van Eemeren and R. Groontendorst (1996) were most prominent.    

     

    Theory Explanation: Simply put, Argumentation Theory is about how people communicate using reason and logic.    

     

    Toulmin's argumentation comprised six interrelated components – Claim, Data, Warrant, Backing, Rebuttal and Qualifier.    

     

    Schellens talks of 'Restricted ' and 'Unrestricted' argumentation.    

     

    Eemeren and Groontendorst lay out the different stages of argument as Confrontation, Opening, Argumentation and Conclusion.  

     

    The structure of an argument is typically as follows: an argument has an internal structure, comprising the following - a set of assumptions or premises - a method of reasoning or deduction and - a conclusion or point. An argument must have at least two premises and one conclusion.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Argumentation is used in practically all kinds of interpersonal and group or mass communication whether spoken, written or visual, and is widely used by academicians in all fields, scientists, professionals, lawmakers, media and advertisers.

  • Attribution Theory


    Theorist: Fritz Heider in 1958    

     

    Theory Development: Based on Attribution theory, Edward E. Jones and Keith Davis in 1965 developed the Correspondent Inference Theory, and Harold Kelly developed the Co-variation Model.    

     

    Theory Explanation: Based in psychology, Attribution Theory is about how people attach meanings to other's behavior and their own. Also, without scientific basis, people tend to attribute their own reasons and casual explanations and judgments to the general social environment, in a manner that helps them cope with events and happenings.   

     

    Heider further classified attribution into –
    internal (pertaining to individual behavior) and
    external (pertaining to situations or social environment) attribution.

     

    Heider called people naïve psychologists trying to attribute their own cause and effect relationships to make sense of the social world.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Attribution Theory helps in social sciences research to understand individual motivations in particular and society's behavior in general.

  • Contagion Theories

     

    Theorist: Gustave Le Bon was the first to theorize about psychology. The theory was later developed in the context of various media related theories.

     

    Theory Explanation: In psychology, Contagion Theory is about infectious attitudes or collective crowd behavior which is swayed by emotions. Le Bon theorized that people tend to surrender to emotional arousal within a crowd and imitate each other often leading to irrational behavior or herding behavior.   

     

    One of the many interpersonal communication theories, in organizations, Contagion Theory pertains to the communication networks that influence the attitudes and behavior of members within that network. Also people sharing similar communication patterns or new media networks tend to display similar attitudes and behaviors.

     

    Application/Relevance: The Contagion Theory applies to any organization with inter-departmental networks or self-interest groups or groups that share the same information technology.

  • Cognitive Dissonance Theory

     

    Theorist: Leon Festinger in 1951

    Theory Explanation: Based in psychology, Cognitive Dissonance Theory is the discomfort one feels when in a given situation, they are faced with having to choose between two conflicting beliefs. The theory suggests that people like to hold all of their beliefs and attitudes in harmony and tend to manipulate them to avoid discomfort or disharmony or inconsistency if they had to choose one over the other. Festinger proposed that so powerful is the motive to maintain cognitive consistency that people tend to sometimes behave irrationally in a situation of cognitive dissonance.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Useful in social sciences research or understanding human behavior in times of crisis.

  • Social Identity Theory

    Theorist: Henri Tajfel propounded this theory in 1979

     

    Theory Explanation: Based in psychology, Social Identity Theory is about an individual's sense of who they are based on the social group they belong to, like the family, the club, a sports team, social class, etc.   

     

    People not only find comfort in 'belonging' to a social group or a similar class of people, but also, in order to perpetuate a sense of self-image, they tend to enhance the status of the group to which they belong. The downside is that people tend to discriminate against the groups that they see as 'other'. In fact, the theory was originally developed to understand the psychological basis of intergroup discrimination.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Social Identity Theory helps to explain prejudices in society, racism, genocide, ethnic clashes, stereotyping, etc. The theory’s relevance is especially seen in understanding online behavior of people on social networking sites.

  • Social Cognitive Theory

     

    Theorist: Albert Bandura advanced this theory in 1986, although the theory has been developed from the 1941 theory of social learning put forward by Neal E. Miller and John Dollard.

     

    Theory Explanation: Social Cognitive Theory sets out a view that people are self-organizing, self-regulating, self-reflecting and proactive beings rather than reactive beings. The theory advances the view that human functioning is a result of the coming together of personal, behavioral and environmental influences. In a more practical sense, the theory may be understood as saying that people learn by watching what others do. Or in other words, it revolves around the process of learning through observation.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Social Cognitive Theory can be applied to areas of public health, education, marketing and mass media.

  • Speech Act Theory

     

    Theorist: Ludwig Wittgenstein and J. L. Austin laid the foundations for the Speech Act Theory, although the contemporary theory is credited to Austin and later to John Searle.

     

    Theory Explanation: Speech Act is a technical term in linguistics and talks about its performative function in language and communication.    

     

    Austin analyzed speech acts on three levels:  
    1) Locutionary Act (an utterance or a general statement that conveys some meaning);  
    2) Illocutionary Act (the performance of an act in uttering something or where the speech itself is an act which have been termed as directives, commissives, expressives and declaratives); and  
    3) Perlocutionary Act (where the intention of speech is to change someone's mind like inspirational or persuasive speech)

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Speech Act Theory helps in understanding organizational language, to improve communication by reducing the scope of misunderstandings, and so on.

  • Expectancy Value Theory

    Theorist: Martin Fishbein developed this theory in the 1970s.

     

    Theory Explanation: The Expectancy Value Theory falls under the larger scope of the Uses and Gratifications Theory. Loosely, it is about expectancies (beliefs) and evaluations.

     

    Fishbein theorizes that people's attitude and behavior towards the world is shaped according to their beliefs and the evaluations based on those beliefs.    

     

    The beliefs are of two kinds:  
    1) what they believe in and 
    2) what they believe about.    

     

    The theory states that people's behavior in response to their beliefs and values is always goal oriented; and where there is a dichotomy of beliefs, they tend to choose the one that has a greater combination of success and value.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: This theory can be used to determine the attitudes and predict the behavior of voters, or used by advertisers to understand what kind of marketing will work best on different people.

  • Uncertainty Reduction Theory

     

    Theorist: Charles Berger and Richard Calabrese developed the Uncertainty Reduction Theory (URT) in 1975

     

    Theory Explanation: URT is an interpersonal communications theory. It talks of how people use various communication tactics when meeting new people or forming new relationships, to reduce the uncertainty about their behavior and what the other person thinks of them.    

     

    There are three ways people seek information about another person to reduce uncertainty:  
    (1) Passive strategies (where we observe the other person without making that person aware of being observed);  
    (2) Active strategies (where we ask others about the person); and  
    (3) Interactive strategies (where we directly approach the person we are interested in)..

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: URT applies to any given social set up or in organizational set ups.

  • Altercasting

    Theorist: The concept of Altercasting was introduced by Eugene A. Weinstein and Paul Deutschberger in 1963.

     

    Theory Explanation: Altercasting is a theory of mass communication with roots in sociology. It is defined as 'projecting an identity'; and deals with the 'roles' individuals play in society, or assume roles that are thrust upon them, and behave in a particular way because of social pressures.    

     

    Thus, there are two types of Altercasting:    
    (1) Manded Altercasting -- is when we tell people who they are and how they are supposed to behave by assigning certain importance to that role (Eg: Teacher-Student);    
    (2) Tact Altercasting – is how we put ourselves in a role which elicits certain expectations from others (Eg: A priest is expected to 'behave' like a priest at all times). .

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: The theory is used extensively in media marketing strategies where through powerful images the audiences are cast in certain roles and behavior.

  • Persuasion Theory

     

    Theorist: Persuasion theories have their roots in Aristotle’s Rhetoric

     

    Theory Explanation: Persuasion is a form of social influence. It is a process of subtly or overtly guiding oneself or another toward the adoption of an idea. It is a form of human communication that is designed to influence others by modifying their beliefs, values or attitudes.

     

    There are various methods of persuasion:

    (1) Reciprocity – Reciprocation produces a sense of obligation and is a powerful tool in persuasion;

    (2) Commitment & Consistency – The concept is that if people commit to something, they are most likely to honor it;

    (3) Social Proof – People are persuaded by what others around them in the society are doing;

    (4) Authority – People have a tendency to believe authoritative figures or experts;

    (5) Liking – People do things simply because they like the person persuading them to do it;

    (6) Scarcity – scarcity or limited availability of something makes it more valuable for people to want it more.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Persuasion is extensively used in media marketing communication.

  • Semiotic Theory

     

    Theorist: Semiotic Theory was developed by Ferdinand de Sassure (1857-1913) and C.S. Peirce (1839-1914). Other notable researchers who worked on Semiotics are Noam Chomsky, Umberto Eco, Roland Barthes and Jean Baudrillard.

     

    Theory Explanation: Semiotics is the theory and description of sign systems. The theory assumes that "all symbolic systems in a culture function like a second-order language or text." This sign system is intrinsic to prevalent cultures and is an irrevocable part of people's daily lives. The signs and their meanings are so encoded in the messages that it is for each individual to decode them for their symbolism taking from the local or universal cultural encyclopedia. The signs thus are culturally symbolic and open to interpretation, and are not based on any rules.

     

    There are three branches of semiotics:

    1) Semantics (relation between signs and things);

    2) Syntactics (relation among signs); and

    3) Pragmatics (relation between signs and their effects on people).

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: Semiotics is used in everyday communication from voice and printed texts to mass media images, music, movies, web content and digital multimedia.

  • Theory of Reasoned Action or Planned Behavior Theory

     

    Theorist: Icek Ajzen and Martin Fishbein formulated the theory in 1975, 1980 from earlier research related to the theory of attitude.

     

    Theory Explanation: With its roots in social psychology, the Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA) deals with human behavior. TRA links attitude, intention and behavior.

     

    It posits that a person’s attitude is formed on the basis of

    1) Behavioral beliefs,

    2) Normative beliefs, and

    3) Control beliefs.

     

    TRA refers to a type of peer pressure where a person participates or intends to participate in a particular behavior because of a strong influence from the people around him.

     

    Theory Application/Relevance: TRA is used in social sciences research.
